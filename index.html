01 Cell Biology & Cell Division
Critical
Q1 · Cell Organelles
Which organelle is responsible for the synthesis of proteins in the cell?
+
Every Year98% likely
Ribosomes are the sites of protein synthesis (translation of mRNA).
Found free in cytoplasm or attached to rough ER.
Mitochondria → ATP (energy) | Golgi → packaging/secretion | Lysosome → intracellular digestion.
Q2 · Plant vs Animal Cell
Which of the following structures is found in a plant cell but NOT in an animal cell?
+
Classic Distinction96% likely
Plant cell exclusives: Cell wall (cellulose), chloroplasts, large central vacuole.
Animal cell exclusives: centrioles, lysosomes (sometimes).
Both have: nucleus, mitochondria, ribosomes, cell membrane, ER, Golgi.
Q3 · Osmosis
A plant cell is placed in a hypertonic solution. What will happen to it?
+
Every Year97% likely
Hypertonic solution (higher solute concentration outside) → water moves OUT of cell by osmosis → cell shrinks → plasmolysis (cell membrane pulls away from cell wall).
Hypotonic solution → water moves IN → turgid (plant cell) or lysis (animal cell).
Isotonic → no net movement → flaccid plant cell.
Q4 · Mitosis
During which stage of mitosis do chromosomes line up at the equatorial plate?
+
Cell Division92% likely
Metaphase: chromosomes align at the equatorial plate — the easiest stage to count chromosomes/karyotype.
Prophase → chromosomes condense. Anaphase → chromatids pull to poles. Telophase → nuclear envelopes reform.
Memory: PMAT — Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase.
Q5 · Meiosis vs Mitosis
Meiosis differs from mitosis in that meiosis:
+
High Frequency93% likely
Meiosis → 4 haploid (n) genetically unique gametes (crossing over creates variation).
Mitosis → 2 diploid (2n) identical daughter cells (growth, repair).
Meiosis occurs in gonads (testes/ovaries). Crossing over in Prophase I is key source of genetic variation.
Q6 · Diffusion
Which of the following factors increases the rate of diffusion across a cell membrane?
+
Membrane Transport88% likely
Rate of diffusion increases with: larger surface area, steeper concentration gradient, higher temperature, thinner membrane, smaller molecule size.
This is Fick's Law: Rate ∝ (Surface area × Concentration gradient) / Membrane thickness.
02 Genetics & Heredity
Critical
Q7 · Monohybrid Cross
In a cross between two heterozygous tall plants (Tt × Tt), what is the expected phenotypic ratio of tall to short offspring?
+
Every Year99% likely
Tt × Tt → TT : Tt : tt = 1:2:1 (genotypic ratio)
Phenotype: TT and Tt are both tall → 3 tall : 1 short
This is Mendel's Law of Segregation. Know this cold — it comes every year.
Q8 · Sex-linked Inheritance
Haemophilia is a sex-linked recessive condition. A carrier mother (X^H X^h) marries a normal father (X^H Y). What fraction of their sons will be haemophilic?
+
Sex Linkage95% likely
Cross: X^H X^h × X^H Y
Sons get Y from father; X from mother: X^H Y (normal) or X^h Y (haemophilic) — 50:50.
1/2 of sons will be haemophilic. Daughters cannot be haemophilic (would need X^h X^h).
Q9 · Blood Groups (ABO)
A man with blood group AB and a woman with blood group O have children. What blood group(s) can their children have?
+
Codominance93% likely
Father AB = I^A I^B; Mother O = ii
Cross: I^A I^B × ii → I^A i (blood group A) and I^B i (blood group B) — equally likely.
Children can only be A or B. Neither AB nor O possible from this cross.
Q10 · Mutation
Sickle cell anaemia is caused by a point mutation in which gene?
+
Mutation88% likely
Sickle cell anaemia: single base substitution in the beta-globin (haemoglobin) gene — glutamic acid → valine at position 6.
This causes HbS (sickle haemoglobin). Homozygous (HbSHbS) = disease; heterozygous (HbAHbS) = sickle cell trait (protective against malaria).
Q11 · DNA Structure
In a DNA molecule, adenine (A) always pairs with:
+
Fundamentals96% likely
Watson-Crick base pairing: A–T (2 hydrogen bonds) and G–C (3 hydrogen bonds).
In RNA: A pairs with U (Uracil) instead of T.
Memory: AT-GC (Adenine-Thymine, Guanine-Cytosine).
Q12 · Dihybrid Cross
In a dihybrid cross between two heterozygous parents (AaBb × AaBb), what is the expected phenotypic ratio assuming independent assortment?
+
Dihybrid90% likely
AaBb × AaBb → 9:3:3:1 (Mendel's Law of Independent Assortment).
9 A_B_ : 3 A_bb : 3 aaB_ : 1 aabb
This ratio is the fingerprint of a dihybrid cross with complete dominance.
03 Nutrition & Digestion
Critical
Q13 · Photosynthesis Equation
What are the products of the light-dependent reaction of photosynthesis?
+
Every Year97% likely
Light-dependent (thylakoid): Light energy splits water (photolysis) → O₂ released; produces ATP and NADPH.
Light-independent (Calvin cycle, stroma): uses ATP + NADPH + CO₂ → G3P → glucose.
Overall: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
Q14 · Digestive Enzymes
Which enzyme is responsible for the digestion of starch in the mouth?
+
Digestion94% likely
Salivary amylase (ptyalin) in saliva hydrolyses starch → maltose.
Pepsin (stomach) → proteins. Lipase (small intestine) → fats. Trypsin (pancreas) → proteins.
Table: Starch→amylase | Protein→pepsin/trypsin | Fat→lipase | Maltose→maltase
Q15 · Vitamins & Deficiency
Deficiency of Vitamin C causes which disease?
+
Nutrition90% likely
Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) deficiency → Scurvy (bleeding gums, poor wound healing).
Vitamin D → Rickets (bone deformity) | Vitamin B1 → Beri-beri | Vitamin B3 (Niacin) → Pellagra
Vitamin A → Night blindness | Vitamin K → poor clotting | Iron → anaemia
Q16 · Food Tests
Which reagent is used to test for the presence of reducing sugars?
+
Food Tests92% likely
Benedict's solution (blue) → brick-red/orange precipitate with reducing sugars (glucose, maltose).
Biuret → purple = proteins | Iodine → blue-black = starch | Sudan III → red = fats/lipids
Non-reducing sugar test: add dilute HCl, heat, neutralise, then Benedict's.
04 Transport Systems in Plants & Animals
Critical
Q17 · Blood Components
Which blood component is primarily responsible for fighting infections?
+
Blood94% likely
White blood cells (leucocytes): immune defence — phagocytosis (neutrophils, macrophages) and antibody production (lymphocytes).
RBCs → carry O₂ (haemoglobin) | Platelets → clotting | Plasma → transports nutrients, hormones, CO₂, waste.
Q18 · Heart Structure
Which chamber of the heart pumps oxygenated blood to the body (systemic circulation)?
+
Circulatory System92% likely
Left ventricle → aorta → body (systemic circulation). Has thickest wall (highest pressure).
Right ventricle → pulmonary artery → lungs (pulmonary circulation).
Blood flow: body → right atrium → right ventricle → lungs → left atrium → left ventricle → body.
Q19 · Plant Transport Tissues
Which vascular tissue transports water and mineral salts from roots to leaves in plants?
+
Plant Biology96% likely
Xylem: transports water + dissolved minerals upward (unidirectional). Dead cells; lignified walls. Movement driven by transpiration pull.
Phloem: transports organic solutes (sucrose) — translocation — bidirectional. Living sieve tube cells.
Q20 · Transpiration
Which of the following conditions will increase the rate of transpiration in plants?
+
Transpiration90% likely
Transpiration rate increases with: high wind speed (removes water vapour from leaf surface), high temperature, low humidity, high light (stomata open wider).
High humidity → reduces diffusion gradient → less transpiration.
Stomata close at night/low light → reduces transpiration.
05 Respiration & Gas Exchange
Critical
Q21 · Aerobic Respiration
What is the overall equation for aerobic respiration?
+
Every Year99% likely
Aerobic: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + 38 ATP
Option A = alcoholic fermentation (yeast). Option C = photosynthesis (reverse). Option D = lactic acid fermentation.
Location: Glycolysis (cytoplasm) → Krebs cycle (mitochondrial matrix) → ETC (inner mitochondrial membrane).
Q22 · Anaerobic Respiration
During vigorous exercise, muscles respire anaerobically producing:
+
Anaerobic90% likely
In human muscles (anaerobic): Glucose → Pyruvate → Lactic acid (causes muscle fatigue/cramps).
In yeast (anaerobic fermentation): Glucose → Pyruvate → Ethanol + CO₂.
Oxygen debt: extra O₂ needed after exercise to oxidise accumulated lactic acid.
Q23 · ATP
ATP (adenosine triphosphate) is described as the "energy currency" of the cell because:
+
Bioenergetics87% likely
ATP → ADP + Pᵢ releases ~30.5 kJ/mol of energy in a form cells can directly use for work (muscle contraction, active transport, synthesis).
Made in: respiration (mitochondria) AND photosynthesis (chloroplasts).
Q24 · Gas Exchange Surface
Which feature of the alveoli makes them efficient for gas exchange?
+
85% likely
Alveoli are efficient because: large surface area (300 million alveoli ≈ 70 m²), one-cell-thick moist walls (short diffusion path), dense capillary network (maintains concentration gradient).
These are the general features required of any efficient gas exchange surface.
06 Excretion & Homeostasis
High
Q25 · Kidney Function
The functional unit of the kidney is the:
+
Every Year98% likely
The nephron is the structural and functional unit of the kidney (~1 million per kidney).
Parts: Glomerulus (filtration) → Bowman's capsule → PCT → Loop of Henle → DCT → collecting duct → urine.
Main functions: Ultrafiltration, selective reabsorption, secretion.
Q26 · Nitrogenous Waste
The main nitrogenous waste product excreted by humans is:
+
Excretion92% likely
Humans excrete urea (made in liver via ornithine cycle from ammonia + CO₂).
Fish → ammonia (toxic, diluted by water). Birds/reptiles → uric acid (conserves water). Mammals → urea (less toxic than ammonia).
Q27 · Homeostasis
The hormone insulin helps maintain blood glucose homeostasis by:
+
Hormones90% likely
Insulin (from beta cells of pancreas): lowers blood glucose — promotes glucose uptake by cells, stimulates glycogenesis (glucose → glycogen in liver/muscles).
Glucagon (alpha cells): raises blood glucose — stimulates glycogenolysis (glycogen → glucose).
Type 1 diabetes = no insulin produced.
07 Reproduction & Development
Critical
Q28 · Pollination
Which of the following is a feature of a wind-pollinated flower?
+
Plant Reproduction90% likely
Wind-pollinated flowers: large feathery stigma (catches airborne pollen), light/smooth/numerous pollen (easily carried), small/dull petals (no need to attract insects), exposed dangling anthers, no nectar/scent.
Insect-pollinated: large coloured petals, nectar, scent, sticky pollen.
Q29 · Fertilisation in Humans
In humans, fertilisation of the egg normally occurs in the:
+
Human Reproduction92% likely
Fertilisation occurs in the upper third of the fallopian tube (oviduct). The zygote then travels to the uterus for implantation (~6–10 days).
Implantation in fallopian tube = ectopic pregnancy (dangerous).
Q30 · Menstrual Cycle
Ovulation in a 28-day menstrual cycle typically occurs on approximately which day?
+
Menstrual Cycle88% likely
Ovulation occurs ~day 14 in a 28-day cycle, triggered by LH surge.
Day 1–5: menstruation | Day 1–14: follicular phase (FSH) | Day 14: ovulation (LH surge) | Day 14–28: luteal phase (progesterone from corpus luteum).
Q31 · Seed Dispersal
Which of the following fruits is adapted for dispersal by animals?
+
82% likely
Animal-dispersed fruits: fleshy/edible (mango, guava) — eaten, seeds excreted elsewhere. Or hooked/sticky (Xanthium) — attaches to fur.
Wind: dandelion, maple (wings), cotton. Water: coconut (buoyant). Explosive: squirting cucumber, rubber tree.
Q32 · STIs
Which of the following sexually transmitted infections is caused by a virus?
+
Health85% likely
HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) → AIDS. Viral STI.
Bacterial STIs: Gonorrhoea (Neisseria gonorrhoeae), Syphilis (Treponema pallidum), Chlamydia (Chlamydia trachomatis).
Bacterial infections can be treated with antibiotics; viral STIs cannot be cured (only managed).
08 Ecology & Environment
Critical
Q33 · Food Chain & Energy
In the food chain: Grass → Grasshopper → Toad → Snake → Eagle
Which organism is the primary consumer?
+
Every Year98% likely
Primary consumer = 1st animal in chain = feeds directly on producers.
Grass = producer | Grasshopper = primary consumer | Toad = secondary | Snake = tertiary | Eagle = quaternary (apex predator).
Energy decreases at each trophic level (~10% transferred).
Q34 · Nitrogen Cycle
Which bacteria are responsible for nitrogen fixation — converting atmospheric N₂ into ammonia?
+
Nutrient Cycles93% likely
Rhizobium (in root nodules of legumes) fixes N₂ → ammonia (symbiotic).
Also: Azotobacter (free-living in soil).
Nitrosomonas → NH₃ to NO₂⁻ (nitrification) | Nitrobacter → NO₂⁻ to NO₃⁻ | Pseudomonas → denitrification (NO₃⁻ → N₂).
Q35 · Population Growth
Which type of population growth produces an S-shaped (sigmoid) curve?
+
Population Ecology88% likely
Logistic (S-shaped / sigmoid) growth: rapid growth initially, then slows as population approaches carrying capacity (K) due to limiting factors (food, space, competition).
J-curve = exponential growth (no limiting factors — unrealistic long-term).
Q36 · Ecological Relationships
The relationship between a tapeworm and its human host is an example of:
+
Symbiosis90% likely
Parasitism: tapeworm benefits (+); human host harmed (−).
Mutualism: both benefit (+/+) — e.g. Rhizobium in legume roots.
Commensalism: one benefits, other unaffected (+/0) — e.g. barnacles on whale.
Predation: predator kills and eats prey (+/−).
Q37 · Environmental Pollution
Eutrophication in water bodies is primarily caused by excess:
+
Environment85% likely
Eutrophication: excess nitrates + phosphates (from farm runoff, sewage) → algal bloom → algae die → bacteria decompose them → use up O₂ → deoxygenation → fish die (dead zone).
It's a chain reaction. JAMB tests both the cause AND the sequence.
09 Evolution, Classification & Adaptation
High
Q38 · Natural Selection
Darwin's theory of evolution by natural selection states that organisms with favourable variations will:
+
Evolution90% likely
Natural selection: individuals with favourable traits survive ("survival of the fittest"), reproduce more, pass traits to offspring → allele frequency changes over generations → evolution.
NOT Lamarckism (acquired traits are NOT inherited). NOT intentional mutation.
Q39 · Classification
What is the correct hierarchical order of biological classification from largest to smallest grouping?
+
Taxonomy92% likely
Correct order (largest→smallest):
Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species
Memory: "King Philip Came Over For Good Soup"
Humans: Animalia → Chordata → Mammalia → Primates → Hominidae → Homo → sapiens.
Q40 · Adaptation
Which of the following is a structural adaptation of xerophytes to reduce water loss?
+
Adaptation88% likely
Xerophytes (plants that survive in dry/arid land e.g. cactus, marram grass) adaptations:
Thick waxy cuticle (reduces evaporation) | Sunken/rolled stomata (trap moist air) | Reduced/spiny leaves (less surface area) | Deep or extensive roots | CAM photosynthesis (open stomata at night).

  • Cell biology fundamentals (organelle functions, osmosis, mitosis/meiosis) account for roughly 6–8 questions. Know the differences between plant/animal cells cold.

  • Memorise all food tests: Benedict's (reducing sugars), Biuret (protein), Iodine (starch), Sudan III (fats). JAMB loves these.
  • Photosynthesis vs. respiration: know BOTH equations, know which organelle each occurs in, know light-dependent vs light-independent stages.
  • Classification mnemonic: King Philip Came Over For Good Soup (Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species).
  • Vitamin deficiency table: Vit A=night blindness, Vit C=scurvy, Vit D=rickets, Vit B1=beri-beri, Vit K=poor clotting. Memorise as a block.
  • Hormones: Insulin lowers blood glucose; Glucagon raises it. ADH controls water reabsorption. Oestrogen/Progesterone control menstrual cycle.
